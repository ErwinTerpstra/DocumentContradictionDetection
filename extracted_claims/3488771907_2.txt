The short story takes place in a land ruled by a semi-barbaric king.
Some of the king's ideas are progressive.
Some of the king's ideas cause people to suffer.
One of the king’s innovations is the use of a public trial by ordeal.
The public trial by ordeal serves as an agent of poetic justice.
Guilt or innocence is decided by the result of chance.
A person accused of a crime is brought into a public arena.
The accused must choose one of two doors.
Behind one door is a lady whom the king has deemed an appropriate match for the accused.
Behind the other door is a fierce, hungry tiger.
Both doors are not soundproofed at all.
The accused can hear what is behind each door.
If the accused chooses the door with the lady behind it, he is innocent.
If the accused chooses the door with the lady behind it, he must immediately marry her.
If the accused chooses the door with the tiger behind it, he is deemed guilty.
If the accused chooses the door with the tiger behind it, he is immediately devoured by the tiger.
The king learns that his daughter has a lover.
The lover is a handsome and brave youth.
The lover is of lower status than the princess.
The king has the lover imprisoned to await trial.
By the time the trial day comes, the princess has used her influence.
The princess has learned the positions of the lady and the tiger behind the two doors.
The princess has discovered that the lady is someone whom she hates.
The princess thinks the lady is a rival for the affections of the accused.
When the accused looks to the princess for help, the princess discreetly indicates the door on his right.
The accused opens the door on his right.
The outcome of the accused's choice is not revealed.
The narrator departs from the story to summarize the princess's state of mind.
The narrator summarizes the princess's thoughts about directing the accused to one fate or the other.
The princess will lose the accused to either death or marriage.
The story ends with the question, "Which came out of the opened door – the lady, or the tiger?"
