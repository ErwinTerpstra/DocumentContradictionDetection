No. 20 Squadron is a Royal Australian Air Force (RAAF) support squadron.
No. 20 Squadron comes under the control of No. 96 Wing.
No. 20 Squadron is responsible for the management of the airfield at RAAF Base Woomera, South Australia.
No. 20 Squadron originated as a maritime patrol unit during World War II.
No. 20 Squadron was raised in August 1941.
No. 20 Squadron operated PBY Catalina and Short Empire flying boats from bases in New Guinea, Queensland, and the Northern Territory.
No. 20 Squadron conducted search-and-rescue missions against Japanese targets in the Pacific theatre.
No. 20 Squadron conducted mine-laying missions against Japanese targets in the Pacific theatre.
No. 20 Squadron conducted anti-submarine missions against Japanese targets in the Pacific theatre.
No. 20 Squadron conducted bombing missions against Japanese targets in the Pacific theatre.
No. 20 Squadron was disbanded in March 1946.
No. 20 Squadron was reactivated as an airfield support squadron in April 2015.
No. 20 Squadron was formed at Port Moresby, New Guinea, on 1 August 1942.
No. 20 Squadron's establishment included six PBY Catalina flying boats and 133 personnel.
Only five aircraft were available initially for No. 20 Squadron.
Only 55 personnel were available initially for No. 20 Squadron.
No. 20 Squadron conducted long-range patrols in conjunction with No. 11 Squadron.
On 18 November 1941, No. 20 Squadron's Catalinas were augmented by two Short Empire flying boats.
On 25 November 1941, one of No. 20 Squadron's Catalinas was dispatched to Western Australia.
One of No. 20 Squadron's Catalinas joined a No. 11 Squadron Catalina in search-and-rescue missions.
No. 20 Squadron found only oil slicks during the search-and-rescue missions.
By the outbreak of war in the Pacific, No. 20 Squadron had a strength of six Catalinas and two Empire flying boats.
No. 20 Squadron's personnel at the beginning of December numbered 14 officers and 118 men.
No. 20 Squadron undertook its first sortie of the Pacific War on 8 December 1941.
A Catalina of No. 20 Squadron located three Japanese luggers in the vicinity of Thursday Island, Queensland.
No. 20 Squadron commenced anti-submarine patrols later in December 1941.
No. 20 Squadron commenced bombing raids against Japanese bases in January 1942.
No. 20 Squadron was responsible for evacuating white civilians from areas threatened by invasion.
On 21 January 1942, one of No. 20 Squadron's Catalinas located the Japanese fleet steaming for Rabaul.
One of No. 20 Squadron's Catalinas signaled a warning to the town's Australian defenders.
One of No. 20 Squadron's Catalinas was shot down by anti-aircraft fire.
The shot down Catalina was No. 20 Squadron's first combat loss.
The Catalinas of Nos. 11 and 20 Squadrons became the RAAF's only offensive weapon against the Japanese.
The raids on Rabaul by Nos. 11 and 20 Squadrons did little to stem the Japanese advance.
Port Moresby was subjected to increasingly frequent attacks in the following months.
The attacks on Port Moresby destroyed aircraft, facilities, and squadron records.
In February 1942, the Short Empires operated by Nos. 11 and 20 Squadrons were transferred to No. 33 (Transport) Squadron.
No. 20 Squadron lost two Catalinas during patrols on 4 and 6 May 1942.
The nine crewmen of the first Catalina lost were later found to have been captured and beheaded.
The crew of the second Catalina lost were captured and subsequently disappeared without trace.
Nos. 11 and 20 Squadrons moved to Bowen, Queensland, on 7 May 1942.
Nos. 11 and 20 Squadrons were soon attacking Japanese targets in Lae, Salamaua, and Rabaul.
On 27 June 1942, each squadron contributed an aircraft to a four-hour raid over Lae and Salamaua.
RAAF crews dropped beer bottles to disrupt enemy soldiers' sleep during the raid.
The sound of beer bottles falling through the air was described as "something between a shrill whistle and a scream."
By 1 July 1942, No. 20 Squadron's strength was six Catalinas and 175 personnel.
No. 20 Squadron had a planned establishment of nine aircraft and 415 personnel.
No. 20 Squadron's prime responsibility in early 1942 was maritime reconnaissance.
No. 20 Squadron conducted reconnaissance as far as New Guinea, the Solomon Islands, and New Caledonia.
The latter half of 1942 saw a greater focus on night bombing for No. 20 Squadron.
No. 20 Squadron relocated to Cairns on 11 November 1942.
From Cairns, No. 20 Squadron continued to conduct reconnaissance operations.
From Cairns, No. 20 Squadron continued to conduct anti-submarine operations.
From Cairns, No. 20 Squadron conducted occasional bombing operations over the waters around New Guinea.
Between December 1942 and March 1943, No. 20 Squadron's aircraft flew a total of 9,629 hours.
Between December 1942 and March 1943, No. 20 Squadron dropped 227 tons of bombs.
No. 20 Squadron commenced mine-laying operations over the Netherlands East Indies in June 1943.
No. 20 Squadron continued to make some bombing raids in June 1943.
No. 20 Squadron continued to conduct supply drops in June 1943.
In September 1944, No. 20 Squadron became part of No. 76 Wing RAAF.
No. 20 Squadron moved to Darwin, Northern Territory, in September 1944.
All three squadrons operated Catalinas in September 1944.
The primary purpose of the three squadrons was mine-laying.
On the night of 30 September 1944, a Catalina of No. 20 Squadron was shot down while attacking a ship at Pomelaa.
Lieutenant Commander P.E. Carr of the Royal Australian Navy was aboard the Catalina that was shot down.
Lieutenant Commander P.E. Carr was captured by the Japanese.
Another of No. 20 Squadron's Catalinas went down on the night of 27/28 January 1945.
The second Catalina possibly went down in a cyclone over the Timor Sea.
The second Catalina was part of the campaign to mine Surabaya.
In March 1945, a detachment of four No. 20 Squadron aircraft laid mines off the coast of southern China and Formossa.
The operations in March 1945 were conducted from Leyte Gulf in the Philippines.
One of No. 20 Squadron's Catalinas was lost on the night of 7/8 March 1945.
The loss of the Catalina on 7/8 March 1945 was most likely owing to bad weather.
Three of No. 20 Squadron's aircraft mined the entrance to Hong Kong harbour on 8 April 1945.
On 26 May 1945, four of No. 20 Squadron's Catalinas mined Wenchow harbour in China.
Wenchow harbour was the furthest north that any Australian aircraft infiltrated during the war in the Pacific.
Three of No. 20 Squadron's aircraft flew the RAAF's last mine-laying mission on 30 July 1945.
No. 20 Squadron's final wartime sortie was a patrol on 14 August 1945.
Following the end of the war, No. 20 Squadron operated in the transport role.
No. 20 Squadron ferried Australian prisoners of war home from various locations in South East Asia.
No. 20 Squadron relocated to RAAF Station Rathmines, New South Wales, on 21 November 1945.
No. 20 Squadron flew its last mission, a transport flight to Balikpapan, on 21 January 1946.
No. 20 Squadron disbanded at Rathmines on 27 March 1946.
No. 20 Squadron was reactivated on 1 April 2015.
No. 20 Squadron was reactivated to support airfield operations at RAAF Base Woomera, South Australia.
No. 20 Squadron consisted of nine uniformed personnel and one Australian Public Service member.
Squadron Leader Simon Bartlett commanded No. 20 Squadron.
No. 20 Squadron formed part of No. 96 Wing.
No. 96 Wing is a component of Combat Support Group (CSG).
The airfield at RAAF Base Woomera had previously been managed under the auspices of Aerospace Operational Support Group.
A command-and-control review commissioned by the Chief of Air Force recommended that the airfield should be administered by CSG.
RAAF Base Woomera was one of two Air Force units formally established on 12 January 2015.
The other unit established on 12 January 2015 was RAAF Woomera Test Range.
The design of the reactivated squadron's crest includes a wedge-tailed eagle.
The wedge-tailed eagle denotes courage and nobility.
The design of the reactivated squadron's crest includes a woomera spear thrower.
The woomera spear thrower symbolizes the town and its indigenous heritage.
The design of the reactivated squadron's crest includes Sturt's Desert Pea.
Sturt's Desert Pea represents South Australia.
The design of the reactivated squadron's crest includes the Pleiades star cluster.
The Pleiades star cluster features in the folklore of the local Kokatha people.
