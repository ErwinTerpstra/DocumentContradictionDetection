Amiral Charner was an armored cruiser built for the French Navy in the 1890s. Amiral Charner was the name ship of her class. Amiral Charner spent most of her career in the Mediterranean. Amiral Charner was sent to China during the Boxer Rebellion of 1900 to 1901. Amiral Charner was assigned to the International Squadron off the island of Crete during the Greco-Turkish War of 1897. Amiral Charner was assigned to protect French interests and citizens. Amiral Charner spent most of the first decade of the 20th century as a training ship. Amiral Charner spent most of the first decade of the 20th century in reserve. Amiral Charner was recommissioned when World War I began in 1914. Amiral Charner escorted convoys for several months. Amiral Charner was assigned to the Eastern Mediterranean to blockade the Ottoman-controlled coast. Amiral Charner helped to rescue several thousand Armenians from Syria during the Armenian Genocide of 1915. Amiral Charner was sunk in early 1916 by a German submarine. Amiral Charner had only a single survivor rescued.
The Amiral Charner-class ships were designed to be smaller than the preceding armored cruiser design. The Amiral Charner-class ships were designed to be cheaper than the preceding armored cruiser design. The Amiral Charner-class ships were intended to fill the commerce-raiding strategy of the Jeune École. The Amiral Charner measured 106.12 meters between perpendiculars. The Amiral Charner had a beam of 14.04 meters. Amiral Charner had a forward draft of 5.55 meters. Amiral Charner drew 6.06 meters aft. Amiral Charner displaced 4,748 metric tons at normal load. Amiral Charner displaced 4,990 metric tons at deep load. The Amiral Charner class had two triple-expansion steam engines. Each steam engine drove a single propeller shaft. Steam for the engines was provided by 16 Belleville boilers. The engines were rated at a total of 8,300 metric horsepower using forced draught. Amiral Charner had a designed speed of 19 knots. During sea trials on 18 July 1895, the engines produced 8,956 metric horsepower. The engines only gave a maximum speed of 18.4 knots. Amiral Charner carried up to 535 metric tons of coal. Amiral Charner could steam for 4,000 nautical miles at a speed of 10 knots. The ships of the Amiral Charner class had a main armament that consisted of two Canon de 194 mm Modèle 1887 guns. The two Canon de 194 mm Modèle 1887 guns were mounted in single gun turrets. One Canon de 194 mm Modèle 1887 gun was mounted fore of the superstructure. One Canon de 194 mm Modèle 1887 gun was mounted aft of the superstructure. The secondary armament comprised six Canon de 138.6 mm Modèle 1887 guns. Each Canon de 138.6 mm Modèle 1887 gun was mounted in a single gun turret on each broadside. Amiral Charner carried four 65-millimeter guns for anti-torpedo boat defense. Amiral Charner carried four 47-millimeter guns for anti-torpedo boat defense. Amiral Charner carried eight 37-millimeter five-barreled revolving Hotchkiss guns for anti-torpedo boat defense. Amiral Charner was armed with four 450-millimeter pivoting torpedo tubes. Two 450-millimeter pivoting torpedo tubes were mounted on each broadside above water. The side of the Amiral Charner class was generally protected by 92 millimeters of steel armor. The steel armor extended from 1.3 meters below the waterline to 2.5 meters above it. The bottom 20 centimeters of armor tapered in thickness. The armor at the ends of the ships thinned to 60 millimeters. The curved protective deck of mild steel had a thickness of 40 millimeters along its centerline. The thickness of the curved protective deck increased to 50 millimeters at its outer edges. A thin splinter deck protected the boiler rooms, engine rooms, and magazines below the protective deck. A watertight internal cofferdam filled with cellulose ran the length of the ship. The watertight internal cofferdam reached a height of 1.2 meters above the waterline. Below the protective deck, the ship was divided by 13 watertight transverse bulkheads. Five more watertight transverse bulkheads were above the protective deck. The conning tower and turrets of Amiral Charner were protected by 92 millimeters of armor.
Amiral Charner was named after Admiral Léonard Charner. Amiral Charner was laid down at the Arsenal de Rochefort with the name of Charner on 15 June 1889. Amiral Charner was launched on 18 March 1893. Amiral Charner was renamed Amiral Charner on 25 March 1895. Amiral Charner was commissioned on 26 August 1895. Amiral Charner was initially assigned to the 2nd Light Division of the Mediterranean Squadron. Amiral Charner was briefly detached for service in the Eastern Mediterranean. Amiral Charner became the flagship of the Higher Naval War College on 6 January 1896. Amiral Charner commanded her sister ship Latouche-Tréville. Amiral Charner commanded the protected cruiser Suchet. The mission of the Higher Naval War College was to prepare officers for command at sea. Amiral Charner was reassigned back to the active fleet on 20 October 1896. Amiral Charner was sent to Crete on 10 February 1897. Amiral Charner was part of the French contingent of the International Squadron deployed in Crete during the Greco-Turkish War. Amiral Charner remained with the squadron until November 1898. Amiral Charner was reassigned to the Higher Naval War College on 1 January 1899. Amiral Charner was reassigned together with the protected cruisers Friant and Davout. Amiral Charner was detached to the Northern Squadron for the first half of the year. Amiral Charner returned to Toulon in late June 1899. Amiral Charner returned to Brest three months later. Amiral Charner was temporarily placed in reserve. Amiral Charner was ordered to Rochefort for repairs to her steam-piping in January 1900. Amiral Charner departed Brest on 26 June 1900. Amiral Charner arrived in Saigon, French Indochina, on 1 August 1900. Amiral Charner supported Allied forces during the later stages of the Boxer Rebellion in mid-1901. Amiral Charner returned to Toulon on 8 November 1901. Amiral Charner was assigned to the 3rd Armored Division on 24 January 1902. Amiral Charner simulated defending against a force breaking into the Mediterranean during the annual naval maneuvers in July to August 1902. Amiral Charner attacked the fortifications at Bizerte, French North Africa, during the annual naval maneuvers in July to August 1902. Amiral Charner blockaded hostile ports during the annual naval maneuvers in July to August 1902. Amiral Charner was placed in reserve in Toulon on 15 January 1903. Amiral Charner was later assigned to the gunnery school in Toulon until the middle of 1910. Amiral Charner became the guardship at Souda Bay, Crete, on 13 May 1910. Amiral Charner was relieved by her sister Bruix in July 1912. Amiral Charner was refitted before being placed in reserve at Bizerta, Tunisia. Amiral Charner was recommissioned when World War I began in August 1914. Amiral Charner was assigned to escort convoys between Morocco and France. Amiral Charner was assigned to escort convoys together with Latouche-Tréville and Bruix. Amiral Charner was assigned to the 3rd Division of the 3rd Squadron based at Port Said, Egypt, in November 1914. Amiral Charner bombarded Ottoman positions on the Syrian coast several times. Amiral Charner ran aground under enemy fire off Dedeagatch, Bulgaria, on 3 March 1915. Amiral Charner had to be pulled off by the small Italian cargo liner SS Bosnia. Amiral Charner was assigned to blockade the coast between Tripoli, Lebanon, and El Arish, Egypt, in late August 1915. Amiral Charner participated in the rescue of 3,000 Armenians north of the Orontes River Delta from pursuing Ottoman troops on 11 to 12 September 1915. Amiral Charner supported the occupation of the island of Kastelorizo on 28 December 1915. Amiral Charner was torpedoed by the German submarine U-21 on the morning of 8 February 1916. Amiral Charner sank in only two minutes. Nearly the entire crew of Amiral Charner was lost. Some 427 men were lost. Only a single survivor was rescued five days later.
