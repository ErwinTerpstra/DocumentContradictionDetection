Talon is a 135-foot (41 m) steel inverted roller coaster at Dorney Park & Wildwater Kingdom in South Whitehall Township, Pennsylvania, United States.
Talon features 4 inversions.
Talon features close to the ground encounters.
Bolliger & Mabillard designed Talon.
Talon was known to be one of the longest inverted roller coasters in the Northeast when Talon opened in 2001.
Talon's track is filled with sand to reduce the noise made by the trains.
On April 28, 2000, Dorney Park announced that a 135-foot-tall (41 m) four inversion inverted roller coaster would be built for the 2001 season.
No further details were given until August 30, 2000.
On August 30, 2000, Dorney Park announced the full details of the new $13 million roller coaster and its name, Talon.
Talon would be the Northeast's longest inverted roller coaster.
Talon would be built on a portion of land set aside for a major attraction by Cedar Fair when Cedar Fair purchased the park in 1992.
Construction of Talon began on September 5, 2000.
Construction of Talon continued through the winter.
The first parts of Talon to be put into place were the brake run and transfer track in October 2000.
The roller coaster was topped off on November 21, 2000.
The highest piece of the lift hill of Talon was topped off on November 21, 2000.
The track of Talon was completed in early 2001.
Talon opened on May 5, 2001 after testing was complete.
The train immediately begins to climb the 135-foot (41 m) lift hill after dispatching from the station.
Once at the top, the train goes through a pre-drop.
The train makes a sharp 120-foot (37 m) downward right turn after the pre-drop.
The train enters a 98-foot (30 m) tall vertical loop after the downward right turn.
The train goes through a zero-gravity roll after exiting the vertical loop.
The train drops back to the ground after the zero-gravity roll.
The train enters an immelmann loop after dropping back to the ground.
The train makes a full 360-degree upward right turn after the immelmann loop.
The train makes a left turn leading into another drop after the 360-degree upward right turn.
The train makes a highly banked right turn into a flat spin after the drop.
The train makes a left turn extremely close to the ground after the flat spin.
The train heads to an airtime after the left turn close to the ground.
The train goes back up after the airtime.
The train leads into the brake run after going back up.
The train makes a right turn into a second, shorter set of brakes after exiting the brake run.
The train enters the station after the second, shorter set of brakes.
One cycle of the ride lasts about 2 minutes.
Talon operates with two steel and fiberglass trains.
Each train has eight cars.
Each car can seat four riders in a single row.
Each train can seat a total of 32 riders.
The train structure of Talon is colored blue and turquoise.
The seats of Talon are black.
The over-the-shoulder restraints of Talon are yellow.
The steel track of Talon is approximately 3,110 feet (950 m) long.
The height of the lift of Talon is approximately 135 feet (41 m) high.
The entire track of Talon weighs just under 3,000,000 pounds (1,400,000 kg).
The track of Talon was manufactured by Clermont Steel Fabricators located in Batavia, Ohio.
The track of Talon is filled with sand to reduce the noise made by the trains.
The track of Talon is painted orange and yellow.
The supports of Talon are blue.
The entrance sign of Talon has a mini-slogan as "The Grip Of Fear."
The word "Talon" is another meaning for "Claw."
The word "Talon" specifically refers to claws on Bald eagles and mythical creatures.
