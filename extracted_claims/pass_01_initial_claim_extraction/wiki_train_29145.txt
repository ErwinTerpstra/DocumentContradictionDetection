State Route 174 (SR 174) is a state highway in the U.S. state of California.
State Route 174 is a two-lane highway.
State Route 174 is 13 miles (21 km) long.
State Route 174 was added to the state highway system in 1933.
State Route 174 connects Interstate 80 in Colfax with SR 20/SR 49 in Grass Valley.
State Route 174 crosses the Bear River next to a 1924 concrete arch bridge.
The majority of State Route 174 is eligible for the State Scenic Highway System.
Local residents have blocked the designation of State Route 174 as a State Scenic Highway due to property rights concerns.
State Route 174 begins at exit 135 of Interstate 80 in Colfax.
State Route 174 turns right on Auburn Street immediately after crossing Interstate 80.
State Route 174 turns right again on Central Street, which bypasses downtown.
A business route follows Auburn Street, Grass Valley Street, and Main Street through downtown.
State Route 174 follows the old Lincoln Highway (US 40) north out of downtown to Rollins Lake Road.
State Route 174 turns northwest along the Colfax Highway after Rollins Lake Road.
State Route 174 crosses the Bear River from Placer County into Nevada County on a 1987 bridge.
The old 1924 bridge across the Bear River is preserved for non-motorized traffic.
The land flattens out as State Route 174 curves north and west through the Empire Mine State Historic Park into Grass Valley.
State Route 174 turns west on Colfax Avenue.
State Route 174 passes under the SR 20/SR 49 freeway.
State Route 174 ends just beyond Auburn Street.
Auburn Street was the former surface routing of SR 49.
The Capital Construction Company began improving the county road between Colfax and Grass Valley under contract on September 29, 1931.
The Capital Construction Company completed the work on the county road by mid-September 1932.
The state legislature placed the county road on the state highway system in 1933.
The county road was an extension of the existing Nevada City-Downieville Route 25.
In the 1964 renumbering, the highway received a signed designation of State Route 174.
Local residents created the Colfax Highway Association in 1967 at the Peardale firehouse.
The Colfax Highway Association was created to preserve the rural nature of the road.
The 21-foot (6.5 m) wide 1924 concrete arch bridge was set for replacement in the late 1980s.
The Colfax Highway Association successfully lobbied to preserve the 1924 concrete arch bridge for non-motorized traffic.
The Colfax Highway Association pushed for Nevada County's Gold Country Stage bus service to add a route along the highway to Colfax.
The Gold Country Stage bus service route to Colfax was agreed to in late 1998.
The Gold Country Stage bus service still runs as Route 12.
Route 12 connects downtown Grass Valley with Colfax's Amtrak station.
The state legislature designated the part of State Route 174 in unincorporated Nevada County as eligible for the State Scenic Highway System in 1991.
The designation of State Route 174 as a State Scenic Highway was attempted by the Colfax Highway Association in 1999.
The Colfax Highway Association argued that the designation would protect the scenic character and rural flavor of the highway.
A newly formed group, Concerned Citizens for 174, opposed the designation of State Route 174 as a State Scenic Highway.
Concerned Citizens for 174 argued that the designation would restrict the property rights of residents along the highway.
The opposition from Concerned Citizens for 174 was successful.
State Route 174 remains eligible for State Scenic Highway status.
State Route 174 is not a State Scenic Highway.
