Maryland Route 704 (MD 704) is a state highway in the U.S. state of Maryland.
Maryland Route 704 is known as Martin Luther King Jr. Highway.
Maryland Route 704 runs 6.53 miles (10.51 km) from Eastern Avenue at the District of Columbia boundary in Seat Pleasant east to MD 450 in Lanham.
Maryland Route 704 is a four- to six-lane divided highway.
Maryland Route 704 connects the northern Prince George's County communities of Seat Pleasant, Landover, Glenarden, and Lanham.
Maryland Route 704 was constructed along the right of way of the abandoned Washington, Baltimore and Annapolis Electric Railway (WB & A) in the early 1940s.
In the late 1950s and early 1960s, Maryland Route 704 served as a temporary routing of U.S. Route 50 (US 50).
The U.S. Highway's freeway was under construction from Washington to Lanham during the late 1950s and early 1960s.
Maryland Route 704 was expanded to a divided highway between Seat Pleasant and US 50 in the late 1960s and early 1970s.
Maryland Route 704 was completed as a divided highway when the portion east of US 50 was expanded in the late 1990s.

Maryland Route 704 follows the abandoned right of way of the defunct WB & A Railway.
The WB & A Railway was an interurban railroad that included a north-south line between Washington and Baltimore.
The WB & A Railway included a branch to Annapolis from Naval Academy Junction in Odenton.
Construction on the electric railway began in 1902.
Service on the electric railway began between the three cities in 1908.
The WB & A Railway went into receivership in 1931.
The WB & A Railway shut down in 1935.
The Maryland State Roads Commission converted the abandoned railroad right of way into a two-lane highway from the District of Columbia line to US 50 (now MD 450) near Lanham between 1942 and 1944.
The WB & A Railway had a pair of timber bridges across its right of way.
The Chapel Road bridge in Seat Pleasant carried what was then MD 389 and is now Addison Road across the railroad.
The Chapel Road bridge in Seat Pleasant was removed.
The junction at the Chapel Road bridge was regraded as an intersection with the new highway.
In 1944, the War Production Board authorized the replacement of MD 202's bridge across the abandoned railroad.
The replacement of MD 202's bridge was one of the few non-war-effort highway projects federally funded during World War II.
Construction on the new steel-and-concrete bridge began in late 1944.
Construction on the new steel-and-concrete bridge was completed by 1946.
Access between the grade-separated highways at the MD 202 – MD 704 junction was via a pair of two-way ramps.
