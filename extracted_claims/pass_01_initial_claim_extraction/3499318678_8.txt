Constant k filters are a type of electronic filter designed using the image method.
Constant k filters are also known as k-type filters.
Constant k filters are the original and simplest filters produced by the image method.
Constant k filters consist of a ladder network of identical sections of passive components.
Constant k filters are historically the first filters that could approach the ideal filter frequency response to within any prescribed limit.
A sufficient number of sections must be added to constant k filters to achieve the desired response.
Constant k filters are rarely considered for modern design.
The principles behind constant k filters have been superseded by other methodologies.
Other methodologies are more accurate in their prediction of filter response than constant k filters.
Constant k filters were invented by George Campbell.
George Campbell published his work on constant k filters in 1922.
George Campbell invented constant k filters some time before 1922.
Otto Zobel was a colleague of George Campbell at AT&T Co.
Otto Zobel was already making improvements to the design of constant k filters by 1922.
George Campbell's filters were far superior to simpler single element circuits used previously.
George Campbell called his filters electric wave filters.
The term electric wave filters later came to mean any filter that passes waves of some frequencies but not others.
Many new forms of wave filters were subsequently invented after George Campbell's filters.
Zobel invented an early and important variation of wave filters called the m-derived filter.
Zobel coined the term constant k for the Campbell filter to distinguish them.
The great advantage of George Campbell's filters over the RL circuit and other simple filters was that they could be designed for any desired degree of stop band rejection.
The steepness of transition between pass band and stop band can be designed in George Campbell's filters.
More filter sections must be added to George Campbell's filters until the desired response is obtained.
George Campbell designed constant k filters for the purpose of separating multiplexed telephone channels on transmission lines.
The subsequent use of constant k filters has been much more widespread than their original purpose.
The design techniques used by George Campbell have largely been superseded.
The ladder topology used by George Campbell with the constant k is still in use today.
Modern filter designs such as the Tchebyscheff filter implement the ladder topology used by George Campbell.
George Campbell provided constant k designs for low-pass filters.
George Campbell provided constant k designs for high-pass filters.
George Campbell provided constant k designs for band-pass filters.
Band-stop filters are possible with constant k designs.
Multiple band filters are also possible with constant k designs.
